# Bold Personas

Composites, not transcripts. Each one is built from a real, cited
interview or research artifact -- named and detailed as an archetype
because a persona has to represent a *class* of buyer, not one person's
literal life story.

## 1. Dalia -- The Property Custodian (PRIMARY MVP BUYER)

**52, custodian officer in the ממונה על הרכוש הממשלתי unit, 14 years in
the role.** Her desk has two stacks: cases she can close this quarter, and
a taller stack she calls "the ones that just sit." She was trained as a
land surveyor before moving into administration, so she still reads a
cadastral map faster than most of her staff -- and it bothers her more
than it should when a case gets closed on paperwork alone because nobody
had time to check the actual historical layer.

**What she's really deciding:** not "who owns this land" -- her actual
call is narrower and colder: *is there provable private ownership, or
does this default to the state by exhaustion.* Every parcel she can't
close stays a liability on a ledger only she really looks at.

**Grounded in:** `buyer-analysis.md` (Buyer 2), `legal-workflow-analysis.md`.

**Quote (constructed, in character, not verbatim):** "I'm not trying to
take anyone's land. I'm trying to stop guessing."

## 2. Yossi -- The Deputy Authority Head (MVP / SCALE BUYER)

**47, סגן רמ"א-equivalent regional deputy, career civil servant, one eye
permanently on the budget cycle.** He inherited a caseload backlog nobody
before him solved either, and he's stopped believing any single hire or
policy memo will fix it. What he actually wants is a number he can put in
front of his own superiors that shows the average time-to-resolution
moving in the right direction, portfolio-wide -- not one dramatic case
study.

**What he's really deciding:** whether a proposed tool changes the
*operational curve* of the whole office, or just makes one lucky case
faster.

**Grounded in:** `buyer-analysis.md` (Buyer 1), Itamar's macro/policy-layer
endorsement in `itamar-water-infra-meeting.md`.

**Quote (constructed):** "Tell me this moves the average, not just the
best case you cherry-picked for the demo."

## 3. Avi -- The Private Claimant (BIG-MARKET EXPANSION)

**61, third-generation on land his family has worked but never formally
registered.** He has boxes of old receipts, a grandfather's testimony he
half-remembers being told as a child, and a lawyer who's told him
honestly that testimony alone won't hold up. He is not choosing between
"pay for evidence" or "don't" -- he's choosing between paying for it now
or finding out too late, in a hearing, that it doesn't exist.

**What he's really deciding:** whether the cost of reconstructing his
evidence (a small fraction of the land's value) is worth paying before
he's forced to discover the gap in front of a committee instead.

**Grounded in:** `buyer-analysis.md` (Buyer 3) -- the failure pattern
this persona embodies is real and on file, referenced without naming the
specific case per the room's standing constraint.

**Quote (constructed):** "I know the land is ours. I need someone who can
prove what I already know."

## 4. Itamar-type -- The Field Engineer (LOW PRIORITY, CONTEXT ONLY)

**Real interview, not a composite -- kept close to the source because the
whole value of this persona is the counterintuitive finding itself.**
Execution-focused infrastructure engineer. Ownership ambiguity is not his
problem to solve -- it's occasionally his *cover*: in a contested
right-of-way situation, not knowing precisely who's behind a claim can be
operationally convenient, not painful. He is the person closest to the
day-to-day land question and the least motivated to resolve it.

**Why he's here anyway:** every "sell to infrastructure" instinct the
room might have needs to be checked against this exact finding before
being repeated. He is proof that "the buyer" inside a sector is not
uniform, and guessing wrong wastes a sales cycle.

**Grounded in:** `itamar-water-infra-meeting.md`.

## 5. Orit-type -- The District Appraiser (VALIDATED, ADJACENT, DEFERRED)

**Real interview, kept close to the source for the same reason as #4.**
Three overlapping roles -- first-objection appraiser, intermediary to
contract appraisers, working appraiser herself. Juggles a dozen-plus
disconnected databases from a personal browser bookmarks folder and has
already half-solved that problem herself. Her real, unmet want is
narrower and sharper: a system that knows what a committee already
decided on a similar fact pattern, so she isn't re-searching her own
archive "hoping to read it someday."

**The signal that matters most:** she already built her own private
Gemini-based assistant rather than use ChatGPT, specifically over
confidentiality concerns -- and she independently reports a colleague
inside the Chief Appraiser's office doing the same. The need is validated
enough that buyer-side users are self-building it already.

**Why she's deferred, not dropped:** her job (precedent/case-law search)
is genuinely different from the core evidentiary-reconstruction job the
MVP is built around -- see
`solution-design/appraiser-vertical-sequencing-decision.md`.

**Grounded in:** `orit-district-appraiser-meeting.md`.

## Reading This Persona Set

Three personas (Dalia, Yossi, Avi) are today's actual product-priority
order. Two (the field engineer, the appraiser) are deliberately kept in
frame as *disconfirming and adjacent* evidence -- proof the room is
resisting the urge to chase every validated pain point at once. A persona
set that only shows you the people you're building for is missing half
its job; the other half is showing you who you're choosing not to build
for yet, and why.
