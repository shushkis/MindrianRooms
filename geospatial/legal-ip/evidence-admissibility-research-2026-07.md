# Evidence Admissibility Research

## The Headline Finding: This Isn't a Novel Evidence Category -- It's Established Precedent

Aerial photographic evidence of land cultivation has direct, high-stakes
precedent in exactly this dispute type: Israel used **1945 British aerial
photographs** to verify whether claimants had begun cultivating land
before the cutoff required to establish the 15-year "prescription"
(adverse-possession-style) period. Per multiple sources, claimants who
hadn't started cultivation by the time of the 1945 photographs lost their
claims -- and other evidence of cultivation was not accepted in place of
the photographic record. This is not a hypothetical analog: it's the same
evidentiary question (proving continuous historical cultivation) the same
jurisdiction has already litigated, at scale, using exactly the data
category HaMuzim reconstructs.

Read carefully, worth naming both halves honestly, not just the
favorable one: the precedent shows the *absence* of photographic
evidence has already been used to deny legitimate claims -- the exact
"lost on an evidentiary technicality" pattern `Avi`'s persona embodies.
That's the strongest possible argument for the tool's value on the
private-claimant side, and it sits in the same politically-charged
territory `market-analyst-report-2026-07.md`'s Read #2 already flagged.

Sources: [Israeli land and property laws -- Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Israeli_land_and_property_laws), [GEOward -- How Aerial Photo Interpretation is Changing the Legal World](https://www.geoward.co.il/en/article/how-aerial-imagery-analysis-create--change-in-legal-world)

## What the Admissibility Standard Actually Is (and Isn't)

No rigid statutory format, certification checklist, or chain-of-custody
protocol specific to aerial/geospatial evidence was found in Israeli law.
Instead, per the one Israel-specific professional source found (GEOward,
an Israeli aerial-imagery-for-legal firm), admissibility in practice rests
on three factors argued case by case, not a fixed technical standard:

1. **Reliability** of the aerial photograph/imagery itself
2. **Qualified expert interpretation** -- "the only way to ensure we're
   dealing with substantiated findings and not assumptions"
3. **Quality of legal presentation** by the lawyer handling the case

**Read:** this is good news structurally, not bad news. A flexible,
reliability-and-expert-weighted standard (closer to common-law practice)
is a *lower* regulatory barrier than a rigid certification regime would
be -- and it means the room's standing architecture decision (descriptive,
not generative; confidence levels; provenance chain, per
`solution-design/initial-exploration.md`) is already built for exactly
this standard. The system doesn't need to satisfy a checklist; it needs
to produce output a qualified expert can stand behind.

Sources: [GEOward](https://www.geoward.co.il/en/article/how-aerial-imagery-analysis-create--change-in-legal-world), [Law of Evidence at Israel](https://lawgratis.com/blog-detail/law-of-evidence-at-israel)
