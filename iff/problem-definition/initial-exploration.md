# Friendly Fire in Dismounted Infantry Operations

## The Current "Solution"
Three methods are in use today:
1. **Operational briefings** -- forces are briefed on who is where before missions
2. **Eye contact / visual recognition** -- soldiers maintain visual on teammates
3. **Fire zones** -- defined areas where you can and cannot shoot

These are not technology solutions. They are cognitive load solutions -- the identification burden is entirely offloaded to human judgment under stress, at range, in degraded conditions.

## Why This Fails
The three methods collapse simultaneously under the conditions where IFF matters most:
- Kinetic contact (the plan no longer holds)
- Low visibility (night, smoke, urban geometry)
- Complex terrain (multiple elevations, non-linear engagements)
- High operational tempo (decisions made in seconds, not minutes)

## Satisfaction / Importance Gap
- **Importance score:** 10/10 -- errors are irreversible, lethal
- **Satisfaction score (current solutions):** ~2/10
- **Gap:** 8 points -- among the largest JTBD gaps possible

## Key Insight from Founder
"Today we brief the forces with who works where, always keep eye contact with friends, we know where can shoot and where can't."

Translation: the current solution is entirely procedural and social. No technology in the loop at the moment of identification.

## Open Questions
- Is the core failure a detection problem or a communication/propagation problem?
- Under what specific conditions (terrain, time, unit size) do current methods most frequently fail?
- What is the actual chain of events in a friendly fire incident -- where does the system break down first?
